Conflict & Team Cohesion

Leading a multicultural utilities team — turning language-based friction into one team

Łukasz Klimowski | Proposal for Teamleader Electrical Utilities · Ciner Glass, Lommel

From my current work on site I can see the team starting to divide along language lines — Dutch-speaking Belgian colleagues, Belgian colleagues speaking Turkish, and English-speaking internationals like me. Left alone, this friction hurts safety, knowledge flow, and reliability. As an English-speaking immigrant I am in none of these factions — which lets me act as a neutral bridge, fair to every group.

1. Diagnose before judging

Language is usually the visible fault line, not the root cause. Before reacting I talk to people one-to-one to understand what's really underneath:

  • Is it genuinely language — or workload fairness, perceived favouritism, unclear ownership of a utility chain?
  • Groups form because information flows in-language; then the fix is structural communication, not forcing people to socialise.
  • Listen first, decide on facts — the same discipline I use with technical faults (RCA), applied to people.

2. The conversation — the SBI model

I run conflict and feedback conversations with SBI — it stays objective and works across cultures because it removes blame and labels:

Situation → Behavior → Impact
StepWhat I say
SituationThe specific when/where — "Yesterday, on the PC-20 handover…"
BehaviorObservable facts, not interpretation — "…the update was given only in one language" (not "you excluded people").
ImpactThe effect on the team/safety/work — "…so two electricians missed an isolation step."
+ InquiryThen I ask their view — "How did you see it?" — so it's a dialogue, not a verdict.

Facts + impact + a genuine question de-escalate far better than opinions or accusations — especially when people are sensitive about language and belonging.

3. Fix it structurally — don't rely on personalities

I do not fight cultural circles; I redirect them through how the work is organised:

What deepens the divide

  • Ambiguous "just sort it out among yourselves"
  • Information shared only in one language
  • Groups always working within their own circle
  • Hidden resentment over who gets which jobs

My structural fixes

  • Agreed language rules: Dutch for official/safety compliance + English as working bridge; visual work instructions so safety is understood by everyone.
  • Mixed mentor pairs + zone rotation — groups interact through real work.
  • Shared KPI board / single source of truth — no group has an information advantage.
  • Transparent workload (data) — kills the "they get the easy jobs" resentment that hides behind language.

4. Unite on a common goal

  • People bond by solving real problems together — a tough commissioning, a fault under pressure. The work itself is the integrator.
  • Recognise shared wins publicly (a recurring fault eliminated, a clean safety month) — belonging shifts from "my language group" to "our utilities team".
  • I lead by example on language: I'm learning Dutch, so I model the effort I ask of others, and I respect Dutch as the host-country and official language while English keeps technical work moving.

5. Know when to escalate

SituationMy response
Normal friction / miscommunicationCoach 1:1, SBI feedback, structural fix — keep it in the team.
Repeated, unresolved conflictDocument facts, joint agreement with clear expectations and follow-up.
Harassment, discrimination, or safety riskZero tolerance — act immediately and involve HR. No exceptions.

Knowing the line between "coach it" and "escalate it" is part of the job — I won't sit on something that crosses into discrimination or endangers people.

One-line answer for the interview: "I diagnose the real cause one-to-one, I address behaviour with facts using SBI — not blame, I fix the system (language rules, mixed pairs, shared board, transparent workload), and I unite the team on safe, reliable utilities. As an international myself I'm a neutral bridge — and I escalate to HR without hesitation if it becomes discrimination or a safety risk."
Why I'm credible here: at COMEX I led 30+ internal and external people, and at Northvolt I worked daily across production, quality, after-sales and warehouse — leading mixed-background groups is what I've actually done, not theory.
