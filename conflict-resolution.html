Conflict & Team Cohesion

Leading a multicultural utilities team — turning language-based friction into one team

Łukasz Klimowski | Proposal for Teamleader Electrical Utilities · Ciner Glass, Lommel

From my current work on site I can see the team starting to divide along language lines — Dutch-speaking Belgian colleagues, Belgian colleagues speaking Turkish, and English-speaking internationals like me. Left alone, this friction hurts safety, knowledge flow, and reliability. As an English-speaking immigrant I am in none of these factions — which lets me act as a neutral bridge, fair to every group.

1. Diagnose before judging

Language is usually the visible fault line, not the root cause. Before reacting I talk to people one-to-one to understand what's really underneath:

  • Is it genuinely language — or workload fairness, perceived favouritism, unclear ownership of a utility chain?
  • Groups form because information flows in-language; then the fix is structural communication, not forcing people to socialise.
  • Active shopfloor presence (Gemba). I don't need to understand every word in the canteen to read the team's dynamic. Conflict rarely starts with words — it shows first in behaviour: reluctance to help lift a heavy component, ostentatious ignoring, groups splitting at lunch, how a handover is (or isn't) done. I react to those symptoms, and I ask about cooperation directly in a neutral language (English) in 1:1s — never about gossip.
  • Listen first, decide on facts — the same discipline I use with technical faults (RCA), applied to people.
Treat team friction like intermittent communication loss in a Profinet network — look for the structural interference, don't just blame the node.

2. The conversation — the SBI model

I run conflict and feedback conversations with SBI — it stays objective and works across cultures because it removes blame and labels:

Situation → Behavior → Impact
StepWhat I say
SituationThe specific when/where — "Yesterday, on the PC-20 handover…"
BehaviorObservable facts, not interpretation — "…the update was given only in one language" (not "you excluded people").
ImpactThe effect on the team/safety/work — "…so two electricians missed an isolation step."
+ InquiryThen I ask their view — "How did you see it?" — so it's a dialogue, not a verdict.

Facts + impact + a genuine question de-escalate far better than opinions or accusations — especially when people are sensitive about language and belonging.

3. Fix it structurally — don't rely on personalities

I do not fight cultural circles; I redirect them through how the work is organised. I also separate two kinds of communication:

  • Operational Safety Language vs systemic communication. In a live emergency I will not block a fast exchange in whatever language clears the fault quickest — speed and safety win. But anything that leaves a subassembly must be transparent: fault reports, official shift handovers, and safety instructions (LOTO) go in a language the whole team understands (Dutch/English). Knowledge of how a machine was fixed must never stay locked inside one language group.
  • I respect everyone's mother tongue (Dutch, Turkish, others) in private conversation — the operational standard protects the whole team; it does not police how people talk among themselves.

What deepens the divide

  • Ambiguous "just sort it out among yourselves"
  • Critical knowledge shared only in one language
  • Groups always working within their own circle
  • Hidden resentment over who gets which jobs

My structural fixes

  • Operational Safety Language for handovers, fault reports and LOTO (Dutch/English) + visual work instructions so safety is understood by everyone.
  • Mixed mentor pairs (applied during planned maintenance to ensure cross-training without hurting breakdown response times).
  • Controlled, phased zone rotation — during planned PMs, not during critical breakdowns — so no zone becomes the "property" of one small group.
  • Shared KPI board / single source of truth — no group has an information advantage.
  • Transparent workload (data) — kills the "they get the easy jobs" resentment that hides behind language.
Why cross-train, not just "send the two best Belgians": for an acute breakdown, pure skill and speed decide — I'll send the fastest pair. But if a zone is kept as the "elite's" private territory and they retire or fall ill, I have a business-continuity problem. Cross-training during PMs is my insurance against staffing gaps — speed today, continuity tomorrow.

4. Unite on a common goal

  • People bond by solving real problems together — a tough commissioning, a fault under pressure. The work itself is the integrator.
  • Recognise shared wins publicly (a recurring fault eliminated, a clean safety month) — belonging shifts from "my language group" to "our utilities team".
  • I lead by example on language: I'm learning Dutch, so I model the effort I ask of others, and I respect Dutch as the host-country and official language while English keeps technical work moving.

5. Keep it professional — facts and data, not heat

On shift we discuss facts, data and KPIs — not accusations. This is exactly what SBI enforces, and it's the same fact-based discipline I use for technical faults.

  • Give space to cool down — then decide on facts. Emotions are legitimate; under hot glass, noise and pressure, nerves can fray. When they do I don't say "be a robot" — I say "I can see you're angry, let's sit for five minutes, get some water," then we return to SBI and the facts.
  • I separate emotion from technical decisions and mutual respect — stress must never dictate how we treat each other or how we keep each other safe.
  • If someone is genuinely struggling, that's handled the right way — a 1:1, a break, or HR / EAP support — never as a shouting match between colleagues.

6. Acute escalation — what I do in the moment

Tensions can boil over on a busy construction site. When they do:

  • Step in immediately and de-escalate — separate the people, lower the temperature; calm and safety first.
  • Never litigate it in front of others — and especially not in front of the external contractors who share this site. Two of our own electricians shouting at each other damages the team's credibility. The maintenance team's professional integrity in front of contractors is part of Ciner Glass's reputation — I protect it.
  • Address it afterwards, one-to-one, with SBI on the facts — then agree concretely how we prevent a repeat.
  • Cover the leadership gap: escalations spike when the lead is away (training, off-site). I'd set a clear deputy / stand-in and an agreed escalation contact, so the floor is never left without a point of authority.
Behaviour red line: aggression or yelling between colleagues is not acceptable — it's a professionalism and safety issue on a shared site. The expectation is stated clearly and applied to everyone equally.

7. Know when to escalate

SituationMy response
Normal friction / miscommunicationCoach 1:1, SBI feedback, structural fix — keep it in the team.
Repeated, unresolved conflictDocument facts, joint agreement with clear expectations and follow-up.
Harassment, discrimination, or safety riskZero tolerance — act immediately and involve HR. No exceptions.

Knowing the line between "coach it" and "escalate it" is part of the job — I won't sit on something that crosses into discrimination or endangers people.

8. The real constraint today — low visibility & one-sided feedback

Honest about the current set-up: technicians collect tools and parts, go into the factory for a couple of hours unsupervised, then come back — the offices are outside the building, and there is no team leader or senior on the floor to step in. Feedback from the floor is low, so a leader often hears only one side of an incident. Any solution has to work with that reality, not pretend it away. How I'd close the gap:

  • Be where the work is — scheduled zone walk-downs inside during the work window, not desk-bound. Presence even part of the time changes behaviour and lets me read it.
  • Two quick touchpoints per job — a short plan huddle when tools/parts are collected, and a 60-second fact-based debrief on return: status, blockers, anything I should know. Feedback becomes a routine, not a confrontation.
  • An on-floor deputy / lead-hand — designate a senior as the point of contact inside, so someone can step in when I can't be there.
  • Make reporting take seconds and feel safe — a short end-of-job log (CMMS / board), used to fix problems and win the team parts and budget, never to punish. Close the loop visibly so people see reporting works for them.
  • Never act on one side — separate 1:1s first; use the timeline, CMMS logs and what others observed to triangulate the facts before I conclude anything.

9. Example dialogue — after a shouting incident

Two electricians — Tom (Dutch-speaking) and Marco (English-speaking) — shouted at each other at the PC-20 panel. I only heard Tom's version, second-hand.

1 · Each one separately — neutral, facts first

TL → Marco: "I heard there was a heated moment at the PC-20 panel. I've only heard part of it and I want your side before I form any view. What happened, from the start?"
Marco: "Tom changed the isolation plan and didn't tell me — I almost worked on a live section."
TL: "So the change wasn't communicated to you, and that put you at risk — that's serious. And the shouting?"
Marco: "Yeah, I lost it. I was scared and angry."
TL: "I understand the fear — being put at risk isn't acceptable. The shouting isn't either, and we'll come back to that. First let me hear Tom."

(Then the same with Tom: he posted the change on the board in Dutch and assumed it was seen.)

2 · Together — facts, root cause, fix, conduct

TL: "Here's what I see: the isolation change was posted, but only in Dutch, so Marco didn't get it — and he ended up exposed to risk. That's a system gap, not just on either of you. From now, every isolation change is confirmed face-to-face and in English on the shared board. Agreed?"
Both: "Agreed."
TL: "Second — the shouting. I get the frustration, but two of us yelling on the floor, in front of contractors, isn't acceptable and keeps no one safe. If something's wrong, you flag it to me or the floor lead immediately. Can I count on that?"
Both: "Yes."
TL: "Good. Marco — thanks for raising the safety issue. Tom — thanks for owning the gap. This is exactly what I want surfaced early, before anyone gets hurt."

The pattern: hear both sides → SBI facts, not blame → find the system root cause → agree the fix and the conduct standard → thank them for surfacing it (so reporting grows).

One-line answer for the interview: "I keep work conversations on facts and KPIs, not emotion (SBI), I diagnose the real cause one-to-one, and I fix the system — Operational Safety Language for handovers/LOTO, mixed pairs and phased rotation during PMs, shared board, transparent workload. Fast exchange is fine in an emergency; anything that leaves the job must be transparent. Aggression or yelling isn't acceptable, especially in front of contractors; I de-escalate in the moment and address it afterwards. As an international myself I'm a neutral bridge — and I escalate to HR without hesitation if it becomes discrimination or a safety risk."
Why I'm credible here: at COMEX I led 30+ internal and external people, and at Northvolt I worked daily across production, quality, after-sales and warehouse — leading mixed-background groups is what I've actually done, not theory.
