Team Cohesion Plan & Conflict Procedure

A ~6-month plan to turn a divided multicultural team into one department — and a concrete procedure for when conflict happens

Łukasz Klimowski | Proposal for Teamleader Electrical Utilities · Ciner Glass, Lommel

This is the implementation plan behind Conflict & Team Cohesion. Part A is a structured ~6-month programme to build cohesion across the Electrical Utilities team; Part B is a step-by-step conflict procedure any engineer can follow. The goal is never to decide who is right — it is to restore effective cooperation and prevent the next conflict. Developed with input from organisational psychologists and intended to run together with HR.

Part A — 6-month cohesion programme

1 · Diagnosis → 2 · Integration workshop → 3 · Co-built programme → 4 · 360° review

Stage 1 — Team diagnosis (individual conversations)

A one-to-one with every team member — not an interrogation, but a partnership: "we can do something about this together — tell me what you think." Each person gets the same questions:

About the work

  • What works well?
  • What gets in the way most?
  • What causes frustration?

About communication

  • How do you receive your tasks?
  • How do you know a job is yours to do?
  • How do you know it was done correctly?

About the team

  • Who do you work best with?
  • Who is hardest to work with?
  • Why?

About the leader

  • What do you need from the teamleader?
  • What should he do more often?
  • What should he do less often?

About conflict

  • Which situations create the most tension?
  • What most often leads to misunderstanding?

The results are analysed and shape Stage 2 — the plan is built on what the team actually says, not on assumptions.

Stage 2 — Department integration / organisational workshop

Needed so every member feels part of one community. The aim is to reduce the weight of cultural difference: "at work I am not a Turk, a Belgian or a Pole — I am a member of the Electrical Utilities department at Ciner Glass." It does not need a 7-day off-site; with HR it can be run on site.

Added value: lower staff turnover and lower onboarding cost.

Stage 3 — Co-build a diversity & conflict-management programme

Built with the team, based on mutual respect. If the team helps create it, it is not something imposed on them — they feel engaged and responsible for the team's relationships. This is not a promise of paradise, but communication effectiveness improves significantly.

Stage 4 — Periodic 360° review

Every 6 months at first, then yearly. Each employee receives feedback from the manager, peers, and a self-assessment; managers are rated by their team. This matters especially here because cultural differences affect communication style (the basis of cooperation), the manager–subordinate relationship, attitudes to time and punctuality/deadlines, and more. It lets me monitor results and correct deviations early.

Part B — Conflict procedure (engineer's step-by-step)

The goal is not to establish who is right — it is to restore effective cooperation and prevent a repeat.

Step 1 — React quickly

Don't ignore conflict. If you see raised voices, mutual accusations, avoidance of cooperation, insulting comments, or repeated complaints between the same people — deal with it as soon as possible. Unresolved conflicts tend to grow.

Step 2 — Separate the people from the problem

Don't judge people; focus on facts. Don't ask "Who's to blame?" — ask "What happened?"

  • ✗ "Why did you two argue again?"   →   ✓ "Describe exactly what happened."
  • ✗ "Who is responsible?"   →   ✓ "Walk me through the sequence of events."

Step 3 — Listen to each side separately first

Hold the conversations individually. Ask each person the same questions:

  1. What happened?
  2. What facts support your version?
  3. What was the impact on the work?
  4. How do you think it can be solved?

Don't interrupt. Don't judge. Don't point to a culprit. Gather information.

Step 4 — Establish the facts

Check: work orders, reports, schedules, technical documentation, change records, witness accounts. Separate fact from opinion.

  • Fact: "The pump was not started by 14:00."
  • Opinion: "He always does everything too late."

Step 5 — Joint meeting

Bring the parties together. Restate the ground rules: we talk about facts, we don't interrupt, we don't insult each other, we look for a solution. Each person states their position; the teamleader keeps the conversation on track.

Step 6 — Look for the systemic cause

Ask: "What in the way work is organised allowed this problem to happen?" Check whether the cause was an unclear instruction, missing information, no clear ownership, no procedure, a language misunderstanding, a cultural difference, or improper behaviour. Most conflicts have more than one cause.

Step 7 — Agree corrective actions

Define who is responsible for what, which actions will be taken, by when, and who will verify completion. Write all agreements down.

Step 8 — Monitor

Return to it after 1–2 weeks: Is the problem solved? Is cooperation working? Are more actions needed? Without monitoring, the same conflicts recur.

Special rules in an international team

  • Don't assume bad intentions.
  • Don't interpret behaviour only through the lens of your own culture.
  • Make sure instructions were actually understood.
  • Don't allow jokes or comments about nationality.
  • Judge behaviour and results of work — never an employee's nationality.
In an international team people may differ in how they communicate. They must not differ in their standards of mutual respect. My job is to create conditions where conflicts are as rare as possible — and, when they appear, to restore cooperation fast.
The principle I lead by: I don't have to be the team's therapist — I have to be a predictable leader. In extreme situations, it is a matter for HR.
