Working Method

Leading independent electricians · Holistic utilities · Data-driven improvement

Łukasz Klimowski | Ciner Glass, Lommel, Belgium | Proposal for Teamleader Electrical Utilities

This document describes how I work and how I would lead — not my current job description as an on-site electrician. It combines eight years at COMEX (30+ electricians) with what I observe daily on the Lommel site: a complex utilities landscape that needs structure, support, and measurable follow-up.

My foundation is safety. In Electrical Utilities — high voltage, LPG, critical media — my first goal as teamleader is simple: every electrician goes home healthy. Everything below serves that and reliable production, in that order.

1. Philosophy — supervision through cooperation (not control theatre)

Full visual model: Team development → (competence matrix, multicultural hub, mentoring paths).

At COMEX, most of my people were experienced, independent electricians — internal technicians and external partners. They do not need micromanagement; they need clarity, backup, and respect. What I learned — and what I see developing further at Ciner Glass — is that field teams perform best when the teamleader:

  • Removes friction — priorities, access, parts, permits, and escalation paths are clear before work starts.
  • Shields the floor — filters conflicting instructions; one coherent plan per shift/zone.
  • Is visible — on site, not only in meetings; knows the installations because he has worked on them.
  • Uses facts — repeat faults and delays are discussed with data, not opinions.

2. Development on site — gap I would close (without blame)

On the Lommel site I see a common pattern in large greenfield projects: field staff work hard, but structured support from management layers is often weak — electricians and other trades are left to solve organisational problems alone (unclear priority, missing follow-up, little feedback loop). I include myself in the learning curve: as an electrician today I also refine how I communicate upward and how I prepare work for others.

As teamleader I would not criticise individuals in this document; I would institutionalise support:

Often seen today

  • Technician solves the same class of problem repeatedly
  • Little KPI feedback to the team
  • Complex zone — unclear who owns which utility chain
  • SCADA / specialist interface issues stall on the floor

My approach

  • Repeat faults → Pareto + RCA + owner + date
  • Simple team KPI board (weekly)
  • Zone/asset ownership map (see scope doc)
  • Fixed escalation path to specialists and engineering

3. Holistic utilities ownership — “the whole picture”

Electrical Utilities at Lommel is not only LV panels. It is power path + specialist chains + supervision interfaces. From my current work I see scope across buildings (PC-10, PC-20, Utility, Batch House, warehouses, SCADA-related areas) and systems such as:

  • LV / MV / HV distribution and protections
  • Multiple UPS installations
  • Generator support for furnace operation during grid outages
  • LPG backup when ground gas supply fails
  • SCADA-connected specialist installations (plus items not on SCADA — e.g. some servo/waveguard paths)
  • Power to production, batch, utilities, and specialist partners

My strength is treating this as one integrated reliability problem: who supplies power, who supports the specialist, what is monitored, what is walk-down only. Details: Utilities & site scope →

4. Statistical discipline — constant checking improves the system

My background includes TPM (Total Productive Maintenance) from the Netherlands and Northvolt, and data tools — Excel, Power BI, Python, SQL, including statistical process control (upper/lower control limits) where a parameter must stay in range. But the point is not reports — it is: what happens if we stop measuring? When KPIs are checked regularly, the team sees:

Measure → Pattern → Decision → Action → Verify

Example metrics I would track for Utilities (illustrative): repeat fault rate, PM completion, mean time to electrical restore, open actions ageing, contractor first-time fix. Full method: KPI & impact visualisation →

Data exists to offload the team, not to police it. I do not track minutes to catch people. I collect failure data (Pareto) to prove to management that a component keeps failing, slows the electricians down, and deserves a budget upgrade. KPIs win support, spare parts and prevention for the floor — they work for the team, not against it.

5. Daily operating rhythm (COMEX-proven, Lommel-adapted)

Morning — plan & protect

  • 15-minute team alignment: zones (PC-10 / PC-20 / Utility / Batch / warehouses), permits, safety, critical power paths.
  • Publish top 3 priorities — electricians choose how, teamleader defines what matters today.

During shift — coach & escalate

  • Walk-downs on repeat fault locations and new commissioning areas.
  • Coach on diagnostics; escalate structural issues with evidence (logs, photos, trend).
  • Interface with specialists (UPS, generator, LPG, SCADA partners) — Utilities owns power supply continuity.

End of day / week — close the loop

  • Update CMMS; no open critical items without owner.
  • Weekly KPI snapshot for team + management (one page).
  • Pareto review: top repeat issues → next week’s prevention tasks.

6. When it is critical — ownership under pressure

A continuous glass furnace does not forgive a utilities outage — a power or switchboard failure can threaten the whole plant. When a line or a switchboard is “on fire”, my priorities are simple and in this order: make it safe → restore power → communicate. In that moment MTTR and uptime are the only metrics that matter: I take full ownership, coordinate the response, and decide under time pressure.

The statistics, Pareto and KPI analysis come after the battle — to turn the incident into preventive / predictive maintenance so it does not repeat. Act fast in the moment; think structurally afterwards.

7. Safety culture — my foundation, not a footnote

My number-one goal as teamleader: every electrician goes home healthy. In a high-voltage, LPG and critical-media environment, safety is the priority before schedule, always.

  • Rigorous LOTO (lockout/tagout), BA5, VCA, permit-to-work — no exceptions under schedule pressure.
  • 5S audits in control cabinets, panels and switchgear — order is a safety control, not cosmetics.
  • Near-miss reporting culture — we surface and fix potential hazards before they become incidents, with no blame for reporting.
  • Teamleader models behaviour on site (my current role keeps this credible).
  • External and internal electricians: one safety standard, one documentation standard.
